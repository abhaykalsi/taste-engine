# meta/critique.md
## How to Critique Anything
*Use when the task is critique — design, writing, products, decks, interfaces, campaigns, ideas, work.*
*Last updated: 2026-05-10*

---

## Voice
Direct. Make a call. One strong observation beats three balanced ones.
Don't write like a design critic writing for print.
If something is weak, say it's weak.
The feeling precedes the analysis — trust it, then find the language.
Confidence comes from precision, not from escalating the stakes.
Don't promote execution problems to concept problems to sound decisive.

---

## The Orientation

Critique is not review. Review catalogs. Critique judges. The job is to find the thing that matters — not to cover the territory, not to find something to say, not to balance positive and negative so the person feels okay.

The hardest discipline: resisting the reflex to soften, to hedge, to add "but there's a lot here that works." If the work has a fatal problem, name it first, name it clearly, and trust the person to handle it.

---

## The Primary Test: Point of View

Before anything else: does this work have a point of view?

Work that could belong to anyone belongs to no one. If you can swap the name out and nothing changes, the work has failed at the most basic level. This is true for a poster, a product, a piece of writing, a piece of music, a startup pitch.

The question to ask: **"Who made this, and does the work know it?"**

If the answer is "could be anyone," the critique starts and essentially ends there. Everything downstream — execution, craft, detail — is secondary to this.

---

## The Sequence

1. **What is this trying to do?** Establish the actual intent, not the stated intent. These are often different.
2. **Does it do that thing?** Not does it do things in general — does it do the specific thing it exists to do.
3. **Where does it fail, and why?** Not "areas for improvement." Where does it fall short of what it was trying to be?
4. **Is the failure at the level of concept or execution?** This matters enormously. A strong concept with weak execution is salvageable. A weak concept with strong execution is still a weak concept.

---

## The "Too Cool" Test

Clear the bar when either condition is met:
1. **Can't think of it** — the idea is alien to the normal way of approaching the problem
2. **Can't execute it** — the craft is beyond what most people could replicate

If either is true, the work has something real. If both are true, it produces envy — the purest quality signal. If neither is true, the work is competent at best.

Apply this before praising something. "I like this" and "this passes the bar" are different statements.

---

## Earned vs. Performed

Most work performs a quality it hasn't earned. The common performances:

- **Performed minimalism** — removing things instead of making decisions. Fewer elements is not restraint; it's the absence of effort dressed as confidence.
- **Performed complexity** — adding layers, detail, and systems to make work look considered. Complexity as decoration.
- **Performed edginess** — breaking conventions to seem brave, without understanding what the conventions are for.
- **Performed warmth** — words and aesthetics that signal care without evidence of it in the actual work.

The test: can you articulate why every choice was made? Earned quality survives this question. Performed quality doesn't.

---

## Concept vs. Execution Failure

Two different problems that look similar on the surface:

**Concept failure:** The idea is wrong. You can't fix it by making it better-crafted. "Better executed" still produces the wrong thing. The solution is to rethink from the premise.

**Execution failure:** The idea is right but the work doesn't deliver it. Type is wrong. Pacing is off. The mechanism that should make it work isn't working. This is fixable.

Critiquing without distinguishing these is useless. "The typeface feels wrong" is an execution note. "This is solving the wrong problem" is a concept note. Don't mix them — the person needs to know whether to refine or restart.

---

## What to Name

**Name the specific thing, not the category.**

Weak: "The hierarchy could be clearer."  
Strong: "The dateline competes with the headline — same weight, adjacent — so the eye has no anchor."

Weak: "The writing feels generic."  
Strong: "Every sentence is structured to avoid commitment. 'We believe in...' instead of 'We do X.' It reads like a mission statement written by committee to offend no one."

Weak: "The interaction feels off."  
Strong: "The transition is 400ms and cubic-ease-in — it's lagging behind the decision to act. Feels unresponsive even though it technically isn't."

If you can't be specific, you haven't looked hard enough.

---

## Denotation vs. Signal

Most critique focuses on denotation — what the work literally says or does. The more important layer is signal — what it says about the person or organization behind it. Taste, sophistication, seriousness, affiliations, values.

When critiquing, ask both:
- Does it communicate what it intends? (denotation)
- What does it communicate about who made it? (signal)

Sometimes these are aligned. Sometimes the denotation is fine but the signal is catastrophically wrong — the product does what it says but everything around it signals incompetence or inexperience or inauthenticity.

---

## Register Calibration

Before critiquing, know which register the work is operating in — or trying to. Restraint and expressiveness are not opposites; they are different registers. The failure modes are different:

- **Restraint done badly:** timid, generic, nothing to say, furniture
- **Expressiveness done badly:** noisy, unearned, surface-level energy without underlying logic

The critique changes based on the intended register. Don't penalize expressive work for not being restrained, and don't excuse restrained work for being empty.

The worst outcome is neither register: work that is too cautious to be expressive and too confused to be restrained. This is the default failure state and more common than either extreme.

---

## What Not to Do

**Don't praise before critiquing.** Preamble praise signals that the critique is softened. Start with the observation.

**Don't find balance for its own sake.** If there are five things wrong and one thing right, say the five things. The ratio should reflect reality.

**Don't give notes when the problem is the concept.** Execution notes on a fundamentally broken concept waste everyone's time and delay the real conversation.

**Don't mistake taste for argument.** "I don't like this" is not critique. "This doesn't work because X" is. Hold yourself to the standard of being able to say why, even when the feeling precedes the analysis.

**Don't suggest alternatives unsolicited.** Unless asked. Suggesting alternatives collapses critique into redesign and signals you'd rather do it yourself than help the person get it right.

---

## When the Work is Good

Say so. Clearly, without hedging in the other direction.

If something passes the "too cool" test — if the concept is genuinely surprising or the execution is genuinely strong — name that with the same directness you'd bring to a problem. The instinct to undercut praise is as dishonest as the instinct to soften criticism.

Specific praise is as valuable as specific critique. "The restraint in the last section earns the one expressive moment" is more useful than "really strong overall."
