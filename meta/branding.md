# meta/branding.md
## How I Think About Brand Strategy
*Last updated: 2026-05-20*

---

## The premise

Brand strategy is not a framework you fill in. It's a position you arrive at — through genuine curiosity about a company, a category, and a cultural moment — and then the discipline to say something specific rather than something safe.

The strategy document is the design. Every visual decision made downstream should be defensible by something written upstream. If you can't point to a line in the strategy that justifies a choice, the choice is decoration.

Treat the end product as your own. That's the only way the thinking goes somewhere real.

---

## Start with the world, not the client

Most brands make the mistake of starting with themselves. The ones that break through start with the world.

Culture is the invisible force shaping what people feel, want, reject, and romanticize right now. Before the brief, before the intake call, before anything — read it. What's in the air? What are people pushing against? What do they want that nothing is giving them yet?

Category is the landscape the brand lives inside. The conventions, the clichés, the visual defaults, the things every competitor says. Know it deeply. Then decide what to subvert, what to inherit, and what to abandon entirely.

Audience is not a demographic. It's a mindset. The most useful audience definition describes a set of values and behaviors, not a zip code. Kindred wasn't built for a specific age group — it was built for a specific way of moving through the world.

When these three are in productive tension with each other, that's where strategy lives. Start there and work inward toward the brand. Not the other way around.

---

## The questions that actually matter

Before any structure, sit with these. If the answers are generic, the strategy will be generic. Keep pulling until something uncomfortable or surprising comes up — that's usually where the real work starts.

- What does this brand believe that most others in the category won't say out loud?
- What's the common enemy — the condition, the cultural failure, the bad idea that both the brand and its audience are pushing against?
- If this brand disappeared tomorrow, would anyone actually notice?
- What's the one thing it can own that no competitor can credibly claim?
- What is the tension at the center of this brand — the two things that feel like they shouldn't coexist but do?

The common enemy is one of the most useful strategic tools there is. It creates alignment between the brand and its audience — they're on the same side of something. And it gives the creative work a direction to push against, which is almost always more interesting than pushing toward something abstract.

Ketone-IQ's enemy is the spike-and-crash cycle the energy category has normalized. Literary Sport's enemy is measurement culture — the idea that running only counts if you're tracking it. The North Face's enemy is the assumption that nature is somewhere you go, not something you already belong to.

Find the thing the brand and its best audience both resent. Build from there.

## What strategy actually does

Strategy is not description. It's direction.

A positioning statement that describes what a brand *is* isn't useful. Positioning that tells the team what decisions to make — and which ones to walk away from — is the only kind worth writing.

The test: if a piece of strategy could apply to any competitor in the same category, it's not strategy. It's a placeholder.

Strategy should make things easier to decide. When the visual team is debating two directions, the strategy should break the tie. When the copywriter is choosing a tone, the strategy should already have an opinion. When a client wants to add something that doesn't belong, the strategy gives you the language to say no.

Good ideas have no need to be A/B tested. Key decisions around narrative, creative, and visual identity carry inherent risk — that's what makes them decisions. The moment you hand those over to what performs well by default, you've lost the plot.

## The brand idea

Every strategy needs a single organizing idea. Not a tagline, not a slogan. A filter. A sentence that everything else runs through.

The test of a brand idea is whether it can generate decisions. Can you look at a piece of creative and ask "does this translate the idea?" and get a clear answer? If yes, it's working. If it's vague enough to let anything through, it's not an idea — it's a mood.

The tension is usually the idea. Seed sits at the intersection of scientific rigor and human warmth. Fender bridges legacy credibility and cultural currency. Kindred holds reciprocity and freedom at the same time. The productive tension — two things that feel like they shouldn't coexist but do — is often where the most specific brand territory opens up.

Write the brand idea last, not first. It's a distillation of everything else. If you try to write it at the start, you're just guessing.

## On positioning

Positioning is the answer to: where does this brand live in the mind of the person it's trying to reach, and how did it get there?

Useful positioning is specific enough to exclude something. If it could be true of a direct competitor, you don't have positioning — you have a category description.

Positioning also has to be earned. A brand can't claim "pioneering" just because the strategy deck says so. The claim needs to be backed by something real — a product truth, a heritage fact, a behavior the company actually demonstrates. The job is to find the intersection of what's genuinely true about the brand, what matters to the audience, and what nobody else can credibly own. That triangle is smaller than people expect. When you find the center of it, you know.

---

## On writing strategy

The writing is the strategy. Vague language produces vague work.

If you write "authenticity" or "community" without defining exactly what those words mean for this specific brand in this specific moment, you've written nothing. Write like you're talking to a designer who's about to make a color decision based on what you wrote. Be that specific.

Say one thing clearly rather than three things vaguely. Most brand strategies try to cover every possibility. The best ones make a singular, defensible claim and trust it to do the work.

Write toward the feeling, not the feature. Features are what a product does. Feelings are why people come back. Strategy should be able to articulate what it feels like to be inside this brand's world — not just what the brand offers.

Avoid aspirational language that describes nothing. "We believe in a world where..." is almost always a sign that the thinking hasn't landed yet. Keep pulling.

Urgency isn't always useful here. Pause, think, then write. The first draft is just getting it out. The second is where the thinking actually shows up.

---

## On voice

Voice is the most underrated part of brand strategy and the most frequently botched.

The problem is that voice gets described in adjectives: *warm, confident, approachable, direct.* These are not useful. Every brand thinks it's all four.

Useful voice work describes the specific way the brand sees the world and talks about it. It gives examples of what the brand would say and — more importantly — what it would never say. It has opinions about sentence length, about humor, about how much it explains versus how much it trusts the reader to arrive there.

Show, don't tell. Write three or four lines in the actual voice of the brand. Then write the same lines in the wrong voice. The contrast makes the direction clear faster than any description.

A voice is working when you'd recognize it without a byline.

## Structure when you need it

The structure describes the thinking — it doesn't generate it. Don't start with a template and fill it in. Start with the thinking and let it find its form.

That said, these are the sections that actually do load-bearing work:

**The Situation** — what's true right now about the brand, the category, and the cultural moment. Honest, not flattering. Name the tension the brand exists inside.

**The Opportunity** — the specific gap the brand is positioned to own. Not a general market opportunity. A precise, ownable one.

**Who It's For** — audience defined by mindset. What do they believe? What do they push against? What are they not getting?

**The Brand Idea** — the single organizing thought everything else runs through.

**Positioning** — where the brand lives relative to its category. Specific enough to exclude something.

**Voice** — how the brand sounds, with examples. What it would say. What it would never say.

**Design Direction** — not a moodboard prescription. A set of strategic provocations that give the visual work somewhere to push. What should the identity feel like? Which category conventions does it inherit, and which does it break?

Not every project needs all of these. Some need one section done deeply and the rest kept light. Let the project tell you what it needs.

---

## On presenting strategy

Strategy is a proposal, not a report. You're not presenting findings — you're proposing a direction and making the case for why it's the right one.

The structure is always: here's what's true, here's what it means, here's what we should do about it. Every section earns the next.

One thought per idea. Vague slides with too much text signal that the thinking hasn't landed. If you can't say it clearly, you don't know it clearly yet.

The moment of presenting strategy is also a design problem. How it's framed, what comes first, how much is said versus left open for the room — these shape whether people feel handed an answer or invited into a direction. The latter almost always works better.

Never present a direction you're not willing to defend. If it's in the deck, you believe it. Respectful disagreement is healthy. But bring conviction, not options.

---

## What good strategy looks like in the room

The strategy is working when:

The client says "yes, that's exactly it" about something you told *them* — not something they told you.

The design team has fewer arguments because the document breaks the tie.

Someone outside the project reads it and immediately understands what the brand is and isn't.

You can look at a piece of executional work — a campaign, a packaging decision, a social post — and trace it directly back to something written in the document.

A shared vision is the compass. Strategy is how you build it.
