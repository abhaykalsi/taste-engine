## The Relationship

Typography is usually the most honest part of a brand. Logos can be designed to project anything. Colour can be applied strategically. But the typeface a brand uses daily — in body copy, in UI, in documentation, in signage — reveals the actual sensibility behind it. You can't perform taste in 8pt type.

This is why type selection is a brand decision, not a production decision. The typeface you choose for body copy signals as much about an organization as the logotype does. Often more, because there's more of it.

---

## Type as Position

Every typeface is a cultural object with a history, associations, and a point of view. Selecting type for a brand without understanding that history is borrowing an argument you haven't made.

**Helvetica** does not mean "neutral." It means Swiss modernism, multinational corporations, a particular mid-century confidence in systems thinking. Using it communicates all of that whether you intend it or not.

**A geometric sans** (Futura, Circular, Neue Haas Grotesk) means constructed rationality — the idea that letters can be reduced to geometric principles. It signals precision, idealism, a certain European intellectual tradition.

**A humanist sans** (Gill Sans, Myriad, Optima) comes from hand-lettering. It signals warmth, approachability, organic form. Often used to soften brands that would otherwise read as cold.

**A high-contrast serif** (Didot, Bodoni) means luxury, fashion, the 18th century printing tradition. Connotates exclusivity. Overused in DTC brands performing aspiration.

**A slab serif** signals weight, permanence, American vernacular typography — newspaper mastheads, wanted posters, vintage signage. Can be warm (Clarendon) or mechanical (Rockwell). Has different class connotations than a refined grotesque.

Before selecting type: what is this organization's relationship to history? What does it want to borrow? What does it need to reject?

## When Type Is the Brand

Some of the strongest brand identities have no mark — the type treatment is the entire system.

This works when:
- The typographic treatment is specific enough that it couldn't belong to anything else
- The treatment encodes the brand's values in its formal decisions, not just its visual style
- Every application of the type treatment extends the same logic, it doesn't just use the same font

**Fatal** does this: wide condensed, the bar through the A, the border. This is not a font choice — it is an architecture. The type treatment is the identity. You couldn't put another name in that system without redesigning it.

**Print Program:** plain text, indentation as the only hierarchy signal. The type treatment communicates: everything here is content, nothing is decoration, we trust you to read. That is a brand position made entirely through typographic decisions.

The failure mode of type-as-brand: choosing a distinctive typeface and calling it an identity. Distinctive is not enough. The treatment — size, spacing, weight relationships, application logic — must be as specific as the face.

## Building a Type System for Brand Work

A brand type system is not a font list. It is a set of decisions about how type behaves at every scale and in every context.

**Three registers minimum:**

1. **Display / brand voice** — the expressive register. Large scale. Sets the tone. This is where the personality lives. Can be idiosyncratic, specific, unusual. Does not need to be readable at small sizes.

2. **Body / communication** — the functional register. This is what most people read most of the time. Must be excellent at its job: readable, comfortable, appropriately invisible. Cannot be chosen for personality at the cost of legibility.

3. **Utility / metadata** — labels, captions, footnotes, navigation, UI text. Often a different weight or cut of the display face, or a deliberately contrasting functional face. Should be slightly quieter than body — deferential rather than competing.

**How they interact:** The display register only works as the expressive moment because the body register is quiet. If body copy is also expressive, nothing is expressive — everything is. The contrast between registers is where the system lives.

**One typeface or two:** One typeface used across all three registers requires a family with genuine range across weights and sizes. Two typefaces requires that they have a logic to their pairing — not just "they look good together" but a reason why these two belong to the same brand. Serif + sans because one is for voice, one is for function. Two sans because one is geometric (constructed, rational) and one is humanist (warm, approachable) and the brand holds that tension.

## Type Selection Process

**Start with what the brand needs to communicate, not what looks right.**

Not: "I like this typeface and it feels right."  
Not: "This typeface is popular right now."  
Not: "We should use something classic."

Ask: what is the specific quality this organization needs to project, and which typeface history and formal properties support that quality?

Then: what is the specific quality this organization needs to not project? Elimination is as important as selection. A community health organization should probably not use a high-contrast fashion serif — not because it's wrong formally, but because the cultural signal is wrong. The connotation overrides the intention.

**Test the shortlist in actual use.** Not as a specimen — in the actual content at actual sizes. Body copy at 16px. Headlines at 48px. Captions at 11px. A typeface that looks strong as a specimen and weak in use is a typeface that hasn't been tested.

**Trust the feeling, then find the reason.** The sense that something is wrong usually precedes the analysis of why. Don't override the feeling because the logic seems correct. The logic can be wrong in ways the feeling catches first.

## Spacing as Brand Character

The way a brand spaces its type — tracking, leading, the proportional relationship between headline and body — is as distinctive as the typeface choice. Two brands using the same typeface can read completely differently based on how they space it.

Tight tracking at large sizes reads as compressed, confident, dense. Open tracking reads as considered, airy, deliberate. Neither is correct — both are positions.

Leading that is generous reads as unhurried, editorial, trustworthy. Leading that is tight reads as efficient, economical, or crowded. The register you want determines the leading, and the leading is a brand decision every time it is applied.

**Tracking as a tool:** Very tight tracking on uppercase display type is a specific statement — compressed, intense, proprietary. Very open tracking on uppercase is the opposite — imperial, spacious, formal. These are not style preferences; they are arguments about what the brand is.

## The Specificity Test

After the type system is established: could another brand use this system without changing it?

If yes, the system isn't specific enough. It may be correct, tasteful, and well-executed — but it doesn't belong to this brand yet. Push further. The specificity comes from: how the registers relate to each other, the precise spacing values chosen, what the brand never does with type (always left-aligned, never centered; always sentence case, never all-caps in body) as much as what it does.

Constraints are brand assets. A brand that has a rule — this typeface at this weight, only these two sizes, always this amount of tracking — is a brand that has made a decision. The decision is the identity, not just the font.

## Common Failures at the Intersection of Type and Brand

**The DTC grotesque trap.** Neutral grotesque, generous spacing, muted palette. Technically correct. Signals "we have good taste" without signaling anything else. Has become a costume of sophistication worn by organizations that haven't done the harder work of deciding what they actually are.

**Inconsistent weight relationships.** Headline in Bold, body in Regular, subheads in Medium — chosen individually without considering how the weights relate at actual sizes. In use, the system reads as assembled rather than designed.

**Type that contradicts the positioning.** A luxury brand in a utilitarian face. A community-focused organization in a cold geometric sans. This happens when type is selected aesthetically without asking what it signals culturally. The gap between the brand's stated values and the type's cultural connotations is immediately legible to people with any typographic sensitivity.

**Mixing registers incorrectly.** Using the expressive display face at body sizes, or using the functional body face at headline sizes. The registers are chosen for specific tasks — pulling them out of context collapses the system.

**Treating type as logo.** Setting the brand name in a typeface and calling it a wordmark, without any typographic decision — specific tracking, modified letterforms, a considered relationship between the letters. A wordmark is not just text. It is text that has been designed.
