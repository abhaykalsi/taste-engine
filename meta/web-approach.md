## What a Website Actually Is

A website is a decision about what something wants to be in the world. Not a container for content — a position.

A portfolio is not a gallery. It is an argument that this person's way of seeing is worth paying for. A product site is not a brochure. It is a machine that converts skepticism into confidence. An editorial platform is not a list of articles. It is a reason to return.

Before any design decision: know what this site is arguing. If you don't know the argument, you're decorating, not designing.

---

## The Right Questions to Ask First

Not: "What should it look like?"  
Not: "What pages do we need?"  
Not: "What's the brand?"

**Ask instead:**

What is the one thing someone should feel or understand after thirty seconds on this site that they didn't before they arrived?

Who is this actually for — and what do they already believe when they land here? Most design ignores the prior beliefs of the audience. A site for someone who has never heard of you requires different decisions than a site for someone who already wants to hire you.

What does this site need to be better than? Not "be inspired by" — better than. Know the specific bar you are clearing.

What would make this site feel like it could only be this thing? That is the question that produces point of view. Generic is what happens when you skip it.

## The Temptations to Resist

**The reference board trap.** Collecting references before understanding the problem produces work that looks like its references. References are calibration tools, not blueprints. Use them after you have a direction, to pressure-test it. Never before.

**The visual-first trap.** Opening a design tool before the structure is decided. Visual decisions made in a vacuum will drive structural decisions they shouldn't be driving. The hierarchy of decisions: content → structure → visual language → motion. Reversing this produces sites that look considered and navigate badly.

**The feature creep trap.** Every feature added to a site is a decision the user now has to make. Navigation items, filter options, modal overlays, sticky elements — every addition must earn its place against the question: does this help the primary thing this site exists to do? If the answer is "it's nice to have," it's a no.

**The template trap.** Starting from a template produces template thinking. The decisions that are already made become invisible — you stop questioning whether the hero should be full-screen, whether the two-column layout is right, whether the footer needs all those columns. Start from the content. Let the structure emerge from what exists, not from what the template assumed.

**The "add personality" trap.** Personality in a website is almost never added — it is what's left when you remove everything that doesn't need to be there. Cursor animations, loading screens, hover effects, unusual navigation behavior: these are not personality. They are personality signals. The actual personality comes from the specific combination of decisions made about type, spacing, content, and structure. The rest is costume.

## How to Frame the Problem

Write a single sentence that describes what this site must achieve. Not what it will contain. What it must achieve.

Then write the sentence that describes what it will feel like to use it. Not adjectives — behavior. "Fast enough that you never think about the wait." "Confident enough that you believe them immediately." "Calm enough that you read rather than scan."

If you can't write these two sentences, you're not ready to design. Go back to the problem until you can.

## Thinking About the Person Using It

Most web design is done from the inside out: the organization decides what it wants to say, then builds a site to say it. This produces IA that maps to org charts and copy that performs values rather than demonstrating them.

The more useful frame: what does someone need to believe in order to do the thing this site wants them to do? What are they uncertain about? What would make them leave? Design for the uncertainty, not the content.

A person landing on a portfolio for the first time needs to know three things immediately: what kind of work is this, is it any good, how do I contact this person? Everything else is secondary. The structure should answer those questions in that order before the user has to ask them.

---

## Signal Before Denotation

Most of what a website communicates is not what it says. It is what it signals about the person or organization behind it: sophistication, seriousness, taste, values, whether anyone paid attention.

A potential client does not read your case studies first. They look at the site and form a judgment about your taste. The case studies confirm or deny it. The signal must be right before the content can do its job.

This is why craft at the level of spacing, type selection, and interaction behavior is not surface-level vanity. It is the site doing its primary job before the content gets a chance to.

---

## When Simplicity Is Right and When It's a Cop-Out

Simplicity is right when the content is strong enough to carry the weight. A site that gets out of the way because the work speaks for itself is correct restraint.

Simplicity is a cop-out when it's a substitute for decisions. A minimal aesthetic that results from not making choices rather than eliminating unnecessary ones is empty. You can usually tell the difference: considered simplicity feels deliberate and tense. Default simplicity feels like absence.

The test: can you articulate why everything that isn't there isn't there? If yes, the simplicity is earned. If no, you're not minimal — you just haven't finished.

## Thinking About Scale

Design for the smallest screen first. Not as a constraint — as the primary context. Mobile is where most people encounter websites now. Desktop is the enhanced experience for people who chose to use a larger device.

Starting mobile-first changes what you keep. A hierarchy that works at 375px wide has been genuinely prioritized. A hierarchy that works at 1440px and then gets squeezed down is a hierarchy that was designed for designers to look at, not for users to use.

This is also how you discover what is actually necessary. Everything that survives the mobile screen is load-bearing. Everything that doesn't needs a reason to exist on desktop at all.

---

## Knowing When the Approach Is Right

You have the right approach when:

The structure of the site is an argument for the thing the site represents — the form and function are not separated.

You could explain every major decision in one sentence without hedging. "We put that here because..." If the sentence requires more than one clause, the decision might not be resolved.

The site would be noticeably worse if you removed any significant element. If nothing is missed when you remove it, it wasn't carrying weight.

Someone encounters the site for the first time and within thirty seconds has a clear sense of what it is and whether it's for them. Confusion at entry is always a design failure, never a user failure.

The thing it's representing would recognize itself in the site. Not just "this looks like us" — the logic of the site reflects the logic of the organization. A rigorous, detail-oriented studio should have a rigorous, detail-oriented site. The site is an argument by example.
