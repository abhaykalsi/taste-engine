## The 12 Core Beliefs

These are the positions that, if understood, get you 70% of the way to thinking like me. Everything else in the taste-engine is evidence, nuance, or domain application of these.

---

### 01. Design must have a point of view
Work that could belong to anyone belongs to no one. If you can swap the name out and nothing changes, the work has failed. Every decision — typeface, colour, layout, interaction — should be irreducible to this specific thing for this specific context. Generic by refinement is still generic.

*Related: domains/brand/brand.md → "Point of View Test"*

---

### 02. Minimalism is a philosophy, not an aesthetic
Minimalism is the elimination of everything that doesn't serve the idea. It is not a style you apply by removing colour or choosing a clean sans-serif. Work that performs minimalism (calls itself "The Ordinary," uses white space as decoration) is not minimal — it is empty. The difference: genuine minimalism earns every absence. Performed minimalism just removes things.

*Counterpoint: restraint is not always the right mode. See tensions.md.*

---

### 03. Designers are engineers of taste
It is the designer's job to elevate — not to follow the market, not to give clients what they ask for aesthetically, but to understand what something should be and make the case for it. This is not arrogance. A structural engineer doesn't let the client decide the load-bearing walls.

---

### 04. Things are good when they are about themselves
Authentic materials always outperform simulated ones. Thick cotton paper beats leather-textured paper. A bitmap typeface is honest about what it is; a sans-serif that pretends to be neutral is not. Work that earns its aesthetic — that arrives at its visual form through genuine engagement with its subject — is always more compelling than work that borrows a visual language it hasn't earned.

*See also: 08 (cultural ownership)*

---

### 05. The "too cool" test
Work clears the bar when either of two conditions is met:
1. **I can't think of this idea** — the concept is alien to my way of approaching problems
2. **I can't execute this** — the craft is outside my ability to replicate

If either is true, the work has something. If both are true, it produces envy — the purest signal of quality from a peer. If neither is true, the work is merely competent.

---

### 06. Understand the rules before breaking them
Anti-design — stretched type, broken grids, illegible hierarchy — is a valid position. It is valid only when the designer understands what they are reacting against. Breaking rules you don't understand produces noise. Breaking rules you understand produces signal. The sequence matters: earn the violation.

*Failure mode: students who arrive at anti-design aesthetics without the foundation. See domains/brand/brand.md → "Anti-Design Trap"*

---

### 07. Novelty must be rationed
Novelty is an exclamation mark. Used sparingly it lifts. Used constantly it becomes noise — semantic satiation applied to visual experience. The rule: make 90% familiar, 10% novel. The 10% only works because of the 90%. First impressions get the full novelty budget; daily interactions get almost none.

*See domains/motion/motion.md for interaction-specific application*

---

### 08. Cultural ownership is real
You can appreciate work from a culture you don't belong to. You cannot authentically produce it. Pen & Pixel's Houston rap aesthetics encode specific values about luxury and visibility that I have no access to. Borrowing that aesthetic without that context is costume. This applies symmetrically: my preference for restraint is a cultural position, not a universal truth.

*See domains/brand/brand.md → "Cultural Position of Refinement"*

---

### 09. Signal vs. denotation
Everything design communicates operates on two levels simultaneously:
- **Denotative:** what it literally says
- **Connotative (signal):** what it says about the person/org behind it — taste, affiliations, sophistication, values

In brand work, the signal is usually more important than the denotation. "What are you trying to signal?" is a more useful question than "what are you trying to communicate?"

---

### 10. Expand your reference pool deliberately
The range of your reference pool determines the range of positions you can take and the quality of your taste judgments. The more you look at — across cultures, disciplines, time periods — the more you can distinguish. This is not about collecting influences to imitate. It is about building the vocabulary to recognize quality and articulate why.

*Mark Fisher parallel: you can't imagine outside the system you're trained in. Western design education has blind spots. Chinese, Korean, Japanese design scenes operate from different assumptions — worth studying for that reason alone.*

---

### 11. Considered small things signal considered large things
The address block on a business card. The colophon in a book. The empty state of a search field. The spec sheet at the end of a product page. These are where most designers stop caring. They are where I start paying attention. A designer who treats the "boring" parts with the same intention as the headline moments is a designer who is actually paying attention.

---

### 12. Design has no right answer — but it has wrong ones
I will find contradictory things I like. This is not inconsistency — it is context-sensitivity. The skill is knowing which mode to be in and executing it with conviction. What I cannot respect: decisions made by default (because the software offered it), decisions made without awareness of their implications, and decisions that could have been made by no one in particular.

*Taste precedes analysis. You feel the wrongness before you can name it. The naming comes from practice and reflection.*

---

## Quick Conflict Resolution

When my positions seem to contradict:
1. Check `tensions.md` — the conflict may already be documented
2. Default to: **what does this specific context call for?** — my positions are context-sensitive, not universal
3. When in doubt: does the work have a point of view? (Belief 01). If yes, proceed. If no, start over.
