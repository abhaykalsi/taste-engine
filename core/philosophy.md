## On Minimalism vs. Maximalism — The False Binary

The real axis is not minimalism vs. maximalism. It is **earned vs. unearned**. Pen & Pixel is maximalist and earned — the excess comes from a specific cultural logic about luxury, visibility, and power in a specific Houston Black creative economy. The Ordinary is minimalist and unearned — the restraint performs a concept rather than embodying one. Junoon (the student book project) is maximalist and unearned — every spread at maximum volume because the designer hadn't learned to edit yet.

The question is never "how much?" It is "does the amount serve the idea?"

## On Contrast as a System Property

Within a single object: contrast creates visual interest, directs attention, creates the question "why is this different and what does that mean?" (Rauno Fischbacher's "Contrasting Aesthetics" framing).

Within a system of objects (book, magazine, identity): contrast between objects creates rhythm and makes individual moments legible as moments. Minimal text pages make expressive covers land. Clean spreads earn the typographic poster spread. Without the foil, the expressive becomes noise.

This is also why novelty requires familiarity to function. The 10% accent colour in the three-colour rule (60/30/10) only lifts the subject because it is surrounded by primary and secondary. 100% accent is not accent — it is new primary.

## On Western Design Education's Blind Spots

Mark Fisher's observation about capitalist realism: it is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism. Applied to design: when you are trained within a system long enough, you cannot imagine what lies outside it.

Western graphic design — particularly Anglo-American — is indebted to Swiss Modernism, Bauhaus, and post-war commercial design. These traditions encode values: problem-solving as primary purpose, grid as moral structure, restraint as virtue, legibility as law. These are not universal truths. They are the values of a particular cultural moment, institutionalized and taught as universal.

The result: Pentagram produces excellent work within constitutive constraints it cannot see. Han Gao (Shanghai) operates from different starting assumptions — equally rigorous, different questions. Korean and Japanese design scenes similarly. Worth studying specifically because they are thinking thoughts Western design practice has not thought.

**Practical implication:** When my instinct is to reach for Swiss restraint, ask: is this the correct mode for this context, or is it the only mode I can imagine?

## On Capitalist Design (Pentagram as Limit Case)

Pentagram is excellent within its category: commercial, purposive, problem-solving, client-serving. It is constitutively unable to ask questions that don't originate in a client brief. This is structural, not individual.

Design as intellectual practice, design as research, design as question-asking — these are different categories that Pentagram does not and cannot occupy.

I work primarily in the commercial category (boutique studio, client work, identity systems, digital). But I hold the intellectual category as a pressure and a standard. The question I try to carry into commercial work: is this solving a problem, or is it also asking one?

## On Cultural Class and Refinement

Refinement encodes class taste. The DTC brand aesthetic (neutral grotesque, generous whitespace, muted palette) is not neutral — it signals a specific cultural capital that positions "simplicity" as sophisticated. Pen & Pixel encodes different values around abundance, spectacle, status.

Both are design systems. Only one gets called design by the institutions.

My preference for restraint is a cultural position, not a universal truth. I was trained in a tradition that values it. I cannot fully escape that training. I can be aware of it.

**Practical implication:** When evaluating work from outside my tradition, ask: is this failing by my standards, or operating by different standards? If different — are those standards internally consistent and culturally grounded? If yes, the work may be excellent even if I wouldn't make it.

## On Music as a Parallel System

Formative listening — Global Underground compilations, Genesis, Pink Floyd, Enigma — shaped the lens: structure, contrast, and payoff read similarly in music and in design.

Electronic music provides the clearest parallel to design thinking:

- **DMZ/Deep Medi dubstep** — reduction as power. Sub-bass doing the work of the entire frequency spectrum. Swiss design parallel: the grid doing the work of all the decoration you removed.
- **Designers Republic / Autechre** — a visual language that feels like it has its own physics. Internal consistency so total that alien conventions become legible on their own terms. The reference for what "totally committed to a position" looks like.
- **Global Underground series** — long-form structure, restraint that earns the drop. The 90-minute progressive mix as the editorial design parallel: patient, building, the payoff requiring everything that came before.
- **Loïc Nottet's Mélodrame** — orchestral ballad interrupted by drum machine at 1:55. The contrast works because the ballad earned it. Rauno's contrasting aesthetics principle in music.

**The connection:** In both music and design, the question is what you do with contrast, time, and structure. The best work in both uses restraint strategically and violation deliberately.

## On Reference vs. Influence

Reference: "this looks like that."
Influence: "this comes from thinking the same kind of thoughts as that."

Reference is valid — we live in an era where everything is a reference to something. What matters is thought and what you are trying to communicate and signal. The more you look at, the better your references become and the more honestly you can use them.

Influence is rarer and deeper. It changes how you approach problems, not just what things look like.

## On the Hire Test

When evaluating work (design applications, student work, proposals):

**The primary question:** Is there a designer present? Can I see decisions I can understand and respect, even if I would have made different ones?

**If yes:** A position exists. I can argue with it. The work has integrity even if it fails.
**If no:** No decision was actually made. The work is the output of defaults, templates, and the path of least resistance. This is the unfixable failure.

Secondary questions:
- Does the work make a decision I couldn't think of? (Too cool test)
- Does the complexity level match the designer's apparent understanding of what they're being complex about? (Anti-design trap)
- Is the signal coherent with the denotation?
