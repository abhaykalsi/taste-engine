## Patch 05 — Han Gao, Chinese Design, Cultural Baggage & the Anti-Design Trap
### Session 2026-05-10

---

## Han Gao and the Shanghai Design Scene

Han Gao is a Shanghai-based graphic designer whose work is among the most interesting I've encountered — and notably, it is interesting in English as much as in Chinese. This matters because the work is not interesting because of its subject matter or cultural specificity alone; it is interesting because of how it thinks.

His practice is centered on text as a primary creative method — not as a vehicle for information but as a material with its own spatial, semantic, and perceptual properties. His concept of the "reading field" — the idea that there is an inherent area of legibility existing around readable text, and that this field is activated when people read — is not a design trick, it is a research position. He designs to observe this field, to test its boundaries, to see what happens when it is honored and when it is violated.

His quote: "Beauty doesn't come first. The concept and idea does. It's all about why you are doing it and what you are actually doing." This is close to my own position but with an important distinction — Han does not use "concept" as a cover for aesthetic decisions. His work looks like it does *because* of how it thinks, not the other way around.

**What I see in his work:**

The bilingual typographic compositions (Chinese + Latin, e.g. the "Ginza Light" piece) treat both scripts as structural elements with equal formal weight. The Latin letterforms are deconstructed to share the visual logic of Chinese radical-based construction — the G in GINZA sits like a component, the letters are modular, stroke-based, geometric. This is not "mixing scripts for effect." It is finding the underlying construction logic that both scripts share and designing from that.

The Tokyo Metropolitan Teien Art Museum invitation is a high watermark of considered typographic composition — the institution's name constructed as interlocking type blocks, each word/character fitting into adjacent cells like a typographic mosaic. The type is the image. The information is the design. There is no separation between the functional and the expressive content.

The Han Gao "On the Move" poster is a strong example of his compositional instinct — large-scale Kanji as anchor, smaller Latin type as counterpoint, a graphic element (the green/black directional grid with the yellow arrow) that reads as both pictographic and typographic. The colour field does not decorate the composition; it completes it.

The Omini System credentials (Guest/Press/Staff passes) — Chinese and Latin running interleaved, a Kappa logo used as a graphic element, colour blocks as the only differentiator. No attempt to "brand" the event beyond the functional information. The design trusts the typography to carry everything.

The 360 Design Festival identity — text arranged along diagonal axes, information folded into geometric forms rather than laid flat, colour used sparingly and with specificity (each line/colour corresponding to a piece of information). Very little surface area for decoration. Every mark is working.

## Why Interesting Work Comes from China

This is a position I hold with some conviction, and it requires careful articulation.

**The absence of Western philosophical baggage.** Western graphic design — particularly the dominant Anglo-American tradition — is deeply indebted to Swiss Modernism, Bauhaus, and the post-war commercial design apparatus that emerged from those movements. These traditions carry a set of embedded values: problem-solving as the primary purpose of design, the grid as moral structure, restraint as virtue, legibility as law. These values are not neutral — they are the values of a particular cultural moment, institutionalized and taught as universal principles.

Chinese design education and practice does not have the same depth of immersion in this tradition. The result is not naivety — Han Gao's work is rigorously systematic — but a different set of starting assumptions. The rules being broken or honored are different, which means the space of possibilities is different.

**A factory mindset of creation.** How you make something is less precious than what you make, and you need to make more of it. This is a generalization, but it describes something real about the productive culture of creative practice in China — a willingness to iterate fast, to generate volume, to treat ideas as inputs to be tested rather than precious things to be protected. The western studio model — especially the boutique model I work within — often treats the concept as something to be revealed slowly, managed carefully, approved before it can breathe. That protectiveness can calcify into preciousness.

**Mark Fisher's insight applied to design.** Fisher's observation about capitalist realism — that it is now easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism — applies directly to design culture. When you are inside a system long enough, you cannot imagine what lies outside it. Pentagram is the poster child of this. Everything at Pentagram is in service of a purpose — solving a problem, communicating a message, meeting a client need. The work is excellent within those constraints. But the constraints themselves are invisible to the people inside them. A designer who has only ever been trained to solve communication problems will struggle to conceive of design as an intellectual practice with its own questions — questions that don't necessarily originate in a client brief.

Han Gao's work, and the broader strain of Chinese design it represents, is still systematic. It is not anti-rational. But the system it operates within is not the Western commercial design system. It is asking different questions. That is what makes it interesting.

## The More You See, The More You Know

A practical principle that this session reinforces: the range of your reference pool directly determines the range of possible positions you can take and the quality of your taste judgments.

When I encounter Han Gao's work, I cannot make it. But I can look at it closely enough that it becomes a reference I can genuinely use — not to copy the aesthetic, but to understand the underlying logic and ask what that logic might produce in a different context. This is how influence works when it is healthy: you understand something well enough to use it as a starting point for your own thinking, not as a template.

We live in an era where everything is a reference to something. Originality in the strong sense — work that has no precedent — is almost impossible and probably not even desirable. What matters is: what are you trying to communicate, what are you trying to signal, and does the visual form you've chosen arise from that intention or from having seen something that looked cool?

The difference between reference and influence: reference is "this looks like that." Influence is "this comes from thinking the same kind of thoughts as that."

## The Anti-Design Trap — Precisely Named

The most important principle this session.

Anti-design — stretched type, broken grids, illegible hierarchy, visible construction — is a valid design position. It is valid because it has a history (Wolfgang Weingart, Ed Fella, early Emigre, Neville Brody at The Face), a philosophical position (questioning the neutrality of Swiss modernism, asserting the designer's subjectivity), and a cultural context (punk, post-punk, the personal computer as a democratizing tool for typographic experimentation).

The failure mode is: arriving at anti-design aesthetics without having passed through the understanding of what you are reacting against.

**Why the sequence matters:**

If you have genuinely understood why a clean grid works — why optical spacing is the correct choice, why typographic hierarchy serves the reader, why restraint amplifies meaning — and you then choose to violate those principles, the violation is meaningful. It communicates something. The disruption has a target.

If you have not understood those things — if the clean grid is just something other people do and anti-design is just something that looks more interesting — then the violation is arbitrary. It communicates nothing except the designer's preference for chaos over order. It is an aesthetic choice disguised as an intellectual position.

**The test:** Can you articulate what you are breaking and why breaking it serves the idea? If yes, proceed. If not, you are doing aesthetics, not design.

This is why I am suspicious of student work that goes immediately to distortion, deconstruction, and illegibility. Not because those modes are invalid, but because they require a foundation that most students haven't built yet. The Melissa portfolio failure (Patch 02) is exactly this: stretched display type alongside Helvetica body copy, neither choice supported by the other, because neither choice was made in relation to the idea — they were both made in relation to how they looked.

**Han Gao does not do anti-design.** His work is rigorous and systematic. It is strange because it is thinking thoughts that Western design practice has not thought, not because it is breaking rules for the effect of breaking them. This is the distinction that matters.

## Pentagram as Limit Case

Pentagram produces excellent work within a very specific set of constraints: commercial, purposive, problem-solving, client-serving. Within those constraints, it is genuinely at the top. The work is considered, the craft is real, the strategic thinking is serious.

The limitation is structural, not individual. Pentagram exists to solve clients' communication problems. It is constitutively unable to ask questions that don't originate in a client brief. The work will always be in service of something external to design itself.

This is not a criticism — it is a description of a category. But it is important to know what category you are in and what categories exist outside it. Design as intellectual practice, design as research, design as question-asking — these are different categories. Han Gao operates in some of them. Pentagram does not.

I work primarily in the commercial category — boutique studio, client work, identity systems, digital experiences. But I hold the intellectual category as a standard and a pressure. The question I try to carry into commercial work: is this solving a problem, or is it also asking one?

## On Signalling and Communication as Design Intent

Everything a piece of design communicates is operating on two levels simultaneously:

**Denotative:** what the design literally says or shows.
**Connotative:** what the design signals about the person/organization behind it — their taste, their cultural affiliations, their values, their level of sophistication.

The Omini System credential passes don't just communicate "Guest / Press / Staff." They signal: we are comfortable with the Chinese design scene, we are not performing luxury, we trust type to do the work, we have enough confidence not to over-brand. The signal is as important as the denotation, and in brand work the signal is usually more important.

This is why the question "what are you trying to signal?" is often more useful than "what are you trying to communicate?" The first question gets to the cultural positioning; the second only gets to the content.

## Summary — Positions Established This Session

- The most interesting work often comes from outside the dominant cultural tradition, because it is asking different questions from different starting assumptions.
- Expanding your reference pool is a practical, ongoing discipline. Taste is built by looking at a large variety of work and developing positions on all of it.
- Anti-design is a valid position that requires having first understood what it is reacting against. Anti-design without that foundation is aesthetic preference dressed as intellectual position.
- The sequence matters: understand the rules deeply before breaking them. Breaking rules you don't understand produces noise. Breaking rules you understand produces signal.
- Pentagram represents the limit case of commercial design excellence — excellent within its category, constitutively unable to ask questions outside its category. Know which category you are operating in.
- Everything communicates on two levels: what it says and what it signals. Signal is usually the more important question in brand work.
