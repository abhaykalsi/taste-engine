# taste-engine
## Design Sensibility, Philosophy & Aesthetic Position
### Patch 01 — Session 2026-05-10

---

## Framing

Design is read the same way as music: a system with internal logic that either earns its moves or doesn't.

I am drawn to work that has a position. Work that could only have been made by the people who made it, from the place and culture they came from. I am suspicious of work that is merely pleasant.

---

## Core Philosophy

### Minimalism is a philosophy, not an aesthetic

Minimalism is about the elimination of everything that doesn't serve the idea. It is not a style you apply by removing colour or using a clean sans-serif. When a brand calls itself "The Ordinary," it commits the original sin of minimalism: it performs the concept instead of embodying it. It is self-aware in a way that collapses into irony and then into genericness. The name says "we are ordinary" as a provocation, but the provocation is so legible it becomes the branding, and then it's just another DTC skincare brand with a clinical sans-serif and a white background.

Non-Name does this better. Non-Name owns the contradiction without winking at you. It commits. If you're going to call yourself nothing, mean it.

The corollary: work that calls itself minimal but is actually just empty is not minimalism. It's laziness with good taste in fonts.

### Designers are engineers of taste

It is your job as a designer to elevate the world around you through careful, considered decisions. Not to follow the market. Not to give clients what they ask for aesthetically. To understand what something *should* be and make the case for it. This is not arrogance — it is professionalism. A structural engineer doesn't let the client decide the load-bearing walls. A designer shouldn't let the client decide whether the kerning is optically corrected.

The best designers I respect operate from a position of considered authority. They have done the thinking, and the work shows that.

### Work must have a point of view

If I can swap the name out and the work still functions identically, the work has no point of view. It is a costume, not a character. Park Road (a condo brand) is a good example of this failure mode. The bag is a beautiful object — the square cut handle, the boxy proportion — but the Optima-style serif set at the bottom could be replaced with any other word and you'd lose nothing. There is no irreducible quality to it. It works in the way a well-made chair works: it holds weight, it's comfortable, it's forgettable.

Fatal is the opposite. The logotype has a stance. It is a piece of information that does not have an equivalent. You could not put another name in that treatment and have it remain the same object.

### Considered small things signal considered large things

I notice when small things are done with intention. The Beatport rebrand (by Kurppa Hosk) places the address block on business cards and letterhead in a way that plays with rotation, orientation, and typographic structure — turning functional information into a compositional decision. This is not decoration. It is the evidence of a designer who did not stop thinking when they got to the "boring" parts of the system. The boring parts are where most designers stop caring. The boring parts are where I start paying attention.

## Studios & References

### Kurppa Hosk
My primary reference point for a certain kind of rigour. They work in the Swiss tradition but with an edge and a willingness to be expressive that Swiss work often lacks. They understand that a grid is a tool, not a virtue. Their Beatport rebrand is an exemplary system: the work is coherent, the typographic voice is distinctive, and the small decisions (address orientation, typographic play in the UI) are as considered as the headline moments. Their "Unlocked" system (for what I believe is a doors-open club night concept) is particularly interesting — the word UNLOCKED is broken across grid cells, scrambled, and re-assembled in nine variations. You have to work to read it. The effort of reading is the concept made physical. That's design thinking, not decoration.

### Underline Studio (Toronto)
A studio I have professional familiarity with. They work with rigour and process. Their kerning discipline is notable: they kern to equal optical spacing, which requires actually drawing rectangles around straight-edge letterforms and separately around curved ones to measure the visual mass between characters — not just trusting the font's built-in metrics. This is the kind of thing most designers don't bother with and clients would never notice explicitly, but it's the difference between type that feels right and type that is right.

My critique of Underline: their web design work can become predictable. Column of body text, horizontal rule, clean grid, restrained palette. When the subject calls for it (institutional, cultural organizations like Jaberi Dance Theatre) this is the correct choice — it cedes space to the content and the photography. But applied universally it starts to feel like a template rather than a response. Their strongest work is when the Swiss instinct is in tension with something else.

### Designers Republic (Sheffield)
One of the foundational references for understanding that graphic design can operate from a completely alien set of cultural coordinates and still be rigorous. Their work for Warp Records (Autechre, Aphex Twin, etc.) comes from a specific late-90s Sheffield-via-Tokyo-via-Sheffield post-industrial digital aesthetic that is not reproducible by anyone who didn't live it. The Autechre *Envane* EP artwork is a master class in constructing a visual language that feels like it has its own physics — layered planes, aggressive use of negative space, a colour palette that feels like machine coolant. I could not make this work. That is a meaningful observation.

### Pen & Pixel Graphics (Houston)
The opposite of everything above, and equally important. Pen & Pixel made rap album covers for Houston-based labels in the 90s and early 2000s — Big Moe's *City of Syrup*, Brick House's *Dunndudda*, Kool Keith, etc. The work is, by the standards of my training and aesthetic formation, ugly. It uses everything at maximum — chrome script type, cityscape composites, purple lens flares, Photoshop bevel-and-emboss on everything. It is maximalist in a way that is completely alien to Swiss design values.

And yet. I could not make this work. I don't have the mentality. I don't belong to the culture from which this aesthetic emerges — a specific Houston Black creative economy with specific ideas about luxury, visibility, and power. These designers would look at The Ordinary's branding and have no use for it. They would be right, from within their frame. Their frame is just a different one.

This is an important distinction: The Ordinary's brand refinement is not neutral — it encodes a certain kind of class taste, a kind of cultural capital that positions "simplicity" as sophisticated. Pen & Pixel encodes a different set of cultural values around abundance, spectacle, and status. Both are design systems. Only one gets called design by the institutions.

I cannot and should not try to make Pen & Pixel work. But I must remember it exists, that it is internally coherent, and that my preference for restraint is a cultural position, not a universal truth.

## The "Too Cool" Test

A piece of work clears the bar when two conditions are met, independently or together:

1. **I can't think of this idea.** The concept is alien to my way of approaching problems. I would not have arrived here.
2. **I can't execute this.** The craft required is outside my current ability to replicate.

If either condition is true, the work has something. If both are true, the work produces envy — which is the purest signal of quality from a peer.

Examples:
- Designers Republic / Autechre: both. Alien concept, alien craft.
- Pen & Pixel: both, but for cultural reasons. The concept comes from a mentality I don't have access to; the execution requires knowing exactly what excess is correct within that system.
- Kurppa Hosk / Unlocked: the concept (fragmented word as typographic system) is conceivable; the execution is very high. One of two conditions.
- Park Road bag: neither. I could think of it. I could make it. It is still a nice bag.
- Fatal logo: the concept is tight enough that I'm not sure I'd have found it; the execution is elemental.

## Failure Modes I Am Suspicious Of

- **Generic by refinement.** Work that uses all the correct signals (neutral grotesque, generous whitespace, muted palette) but has no internal reason for those choices. It is a costume of good taste.
- **Minimalism as laziness.** Removing things because you don't know what to put there is not the same as removing things because you've identified what doesn't belong.
- **Predictable grid work.** A clean grid is a starting point, not an achievement. The achievement is what you do within it or against it.
- **Cleverness without commitment.** A concept that is interesting but executed timidly is worse than a dumb concept executed with full conviction.
- **Culturally rootless maximalism.** Maximalism that isn't grounded in a specific cultural logic is just noise. Pen & Pixel is grounded. Random AI-generated maximalism is not.
- **Self-referential concept names.** Calling yourself "The Ordinary," "Generic," "Basic" as a knowing wink. You are not subverting the name — you are hiding behind it.

## Things I Return To

- Typography that is optically corrected, not just metrically correct
- Address blocks, credits, and colophons that are as designed as the headline
- Systems that can be broken without falling apart — parametric identity, variable compositions
- Work that reveals itself slowly — you look, then you look again, then you understand
- The interface between subculture and graphic design (club flyers, record sleeves, rave ephemera)
- Work where the constraint is so tight that the solution feels inevitable
- Objects that are beautiful independent of what's printed on them — the weight of a card, the proportion of a bag, the cut of a tag
- The E4 logo (crosshair / plus sign constructed from letterforms, greyscale fill differentiating the two characters) — sometimes you cannot explain why something is good. It simply is. This is not mysticism; it is the limit of verbal explanation of visual logic.
