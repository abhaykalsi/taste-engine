## Optical vs. Metric

Always kern optically, not metrically. Metric kerning uses the font's built-in spacing values. Optical kerning corrects for how the eye actually reads mass and space between letterforms.

**Underline Studio's discipline:** Draw a rectangle around straight-edge letterforms to measure equal visual mass between them. Draw a separate rectangle around curved edges. The two rectangles will have different physical measurements — that is the optical correction. Type that is metrically correct and optically wrong is still wrong.

## Hierarchy — Felt Before Understood

Good typographic hierarchy is felt before it is understood. The reading order is clear without a label telling you what to read first.

The tools, in order of preference:
1. **Scale** — the primary signal. Larger = more important. The size differential must be meaningful, not incremental. A 20pt headline next to 18pt body reads as two bodies.
2. **Weight** — secondary. Bold for subheads, lighter for body. Don't use weight as a substitute for scale.
3. **Opacity/contrast** — subtle. Reducing opacity on secondary text creates visual grades without adding colour.
4. **Position** — where things sit on the page implies their relationship to each other.
5. **Colour** — last resort for hierarchy. Colour is better used for wayfinding (this section = this colour) than hierarchy.

**The spacing error:** Spacing between paragraphs wider than spacing between heading and body is a hierarchy failure. The heading and its body are one unit — the tightest spatial relationship on the page. The gap between units (paragraphs) is the breath. When this is inverted, the page reads as isolated blocks rather than flowing document.

## Alignment — A Position

**Left align body copy by default.** Consistent left edge gives the eye a reliable return point. Do not override without a reason.

**Right align body copy almost never.** The ragged left edge interrupts the return movement and adds cognitive load to every line. Right alignment in body context signals: I noticed this option existed and wanted to use it. The exception: very short text strings used as display elements, or specific compositional situations where text is aligned to a structural element on its right side. Even then: one-off, not a system.

**Justified — use only if you mean it, and manage the rivers.** Justified has a specific feeling — formal, archival, continental. When you choose it, you take on the responsibility of managing it: controlling hyphenation, checking for rivers (vertical white space formed by inconsistent word spacing across adjacent lines), ensuring column width is appropriate for type size. Justified text that hasn't been managed looks like a template choice. Not because you can — because it serves the idea.

## Type Selection

**For client work where budget or licensing limits choices:** Inter is the correct practical grotesque. It performs at a high level across weights and sizes, is optically well-corrected, and reads as considered without the premium price of Söhne, Graphik, or Aktiv Grotesk. I advocate for Inter in this context over lesser free grotesques.

**For display/heading work:** Variable optical sizing is worth investing in. A typeface that adjusts stroke contrast and spacing for large display sizes vs. small text sizes is always better than one that doesn't. This is the difference between type that looks right at 120pt and type that works at 120pt.

**Serif considerations:** Prefer serifs with less stroke contrast for UI and digital contexts. High-contrast serifs (Didone, transitional) render poorly at small sizes and on screen. A low-contrast serif (e.g. Freight Text, Tiempos Text) or variable optical size serif is the correct choice for screen-first editorial. WePresent uses this correctly.

**Never use a font because it is available.** Use it because it is the right tool for this specific job.

## The Grid as Composition, Not Structure

The grid is a starting point, not an end state. Something must be slightly unexpected in the arrangement for it to be interesting — not arbitrary, but not predictable either. What I'd call a mild brutalist quality: the logic is legible but the outcome is not obvious.

When the grid is arranged with genuine intention, white space and type have equal compositional weight. The empty areas of the page are as designed as the type.

**Good examples from this session:**
- Stone Lullaby tracklist: text occupying a small corner of a very large field of white. The proportions are the design.
- Print Program website: indentation as the only hierarchy signal. Three typographic registers, no decoration.
- Müller-Brockmann business card: exact emptiness. Not generous minimalism — precise.
- Four Hundred Coffee card: GPS coordinates as the unexpected detail. Arises from the information itself, not applied from outside.

## On Colophons, Credits, and Metadata

These deserve the same attention as the headline. A designer who treats the boring parts with the same intention as the primary content is a designer who is actually paying attention.

The credits on a record sleeve. The colophon in a book. The address block on a business card. The metadata in an article byline. These are where I start paying attention when everything else is correct.

## Contrast in Type Systems

Within a book, magazine, or identity system: typographic register should vary across pieces. Minimal text pages make expressive covers legible as expressive. Clean spreads earn the typographic poster spread.

Three registers is usually the right number for a type system:
1. Display/headline — large, expressive, the "voice"
2. Body/reading — optimized for reading, should disappear
3. Utility/metadata — small, functional, often a different typeface entirely

These should be selected as a system, not independently. The question is how they work together under constraint, not how each one looks in isolation.
